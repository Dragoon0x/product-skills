# Tier Design Craft
**Purpose:** Apply tier design craft with the strategic depth, analytical rigor, and execution discipline that separates product teams that ship outcomes from product teams that ship features — where every decision is backed by evidence, connected to strategy, and measurable in impact.

## Why This Matters

Conversion and monetization are where product value translates into business value — the pricing model, the packaging strategy, the upgrade triggers, the landing page that converts visitors, and the checkout flow that captures revenue. These decisions are product decisions, not marketing decisions, because they're embedded in the product experience. A pricing page IS a product page. An upgrade prompt IS a product interaction. The PM who treats monetization as 'someone else's problem' is giving away the most impactful lever in the business.

For tier design craft specifically, this is one of the product decisions that compounds — get it right and everything downstream works better. Get it wrong and the team spends months building confidently in the wrong direction. The difference between high-performing product teams and struggling ones is usually 5-10 decisions like this one, made with frameworks instead of gut feeling.

## The Frameworks

Conversion Psychology: (1) Loss aversion — people fear losing more than they desire gaining. Frame the CTA as avoiding loss: 'Don't miss out' > 'Sign up.' (2) Social proof — people follow others. Show usage numbers, testimonials, logos. (3) Anchoring — the first number sets the reference. Show the highest price first so the middle tier feels reasonable. (4) Friction reduction — every field, every click, every decision is friction. Remove everything unnecessary. (5) Urgency — genuine urgency accelerates decisions. Fake urgency destroys trust.

Value-Based Pricing Framework: Price = f(value delivered), not f(cost to build). Step 1: Identify the value metric — the unit that scales with user value (seats, usage, transactions, storage). Step 2: Quantify the value — if the product saves 10 hours/week at $50/hour, the value is $500/week or $26K/year. Step 3: Price at 10-20% of value — a $26K value justifies a $2.6K-$5.2K annual price. Step 4: Test willingness to pay through Van Westendorp (at what price is this product too expensive? too cheap? expensive but worth it? a bargain?). Most B2B SaaS is underpriced by 30-50% because founders price on gut feeling, not value analysis.

Good/Better/Best Packaging: Three tiers that serve three segments. Good (starter): minimum viable product at a price that removes all objections — the entry point. Better (professional): the ICP tier — 60-70% of customers should land here. Best (enterprise): premium tier with white-glove features. Each tier needs a clear 'upgrade unlock' — the ONE feature valuable enough to justify the price jump. If there's no clear unlock, the tiers are arbitrary. The center-stage effect ensures the middle tier gets the most attention — design it to be your target tier.

## Mental Models for Product Thinking

**The Two-Way Door Test (Bezos):** Is this decision reversible? If yes (two-way door), decide quickly — you can always walk back. If no (one-way door), decide carefully. Most product decisions are two-way doors that teams treat as one-way doors, causing unnecessary paralysis. Pricing, positioning, and architecture decisions are one-way doors. Feature decisions, copy changes, and experiment designs are almost always two-way doors.

**First Principles Thinking:** Strip the problem to its fundamental truths and reason up from there. Not 'what does the competitor do?' but 'what does the user actually need?' Not 'what's the industry standard?' but 'what would we build if no standard existed?' First principles thinking produces original solutions. Analogical thinking produces copies.

**The Inversion Technique (Munger):** Instead of asking 'how do I succeed at tier design craft?' ask 'how would I definitely fail?' Then avoid those failure modes. Common inversions: How would I definitely ship a bad product? (Skip user research, ignore feedback, optimize for stakeholder approval.) How would I definitely miss the market? (Build for the average user instead of the passionate early adopter.)

**The Regret Minimization Framework (Bezos):** When deciding, ask: 'In 10 years, will I regret not doing this?' If the opportunity is rare, the learning irreplaceable, or the market window closing — bias toward action. If the opportunity will come again, the decision is reversible, or the stakes are low — bias toward patience.

## How to Execute

**Step 1: Diagnose.** Before changing anything, understand the current state. What does the data say? What do users say? Where's the gap between assumption and reality? Diagnosis prevents solving the wrong problem.

**Step 2: Hypothesize.** Form a specific hypothesis: 'We believe [change] will produce [measurable outcome] because [mechanism].' All three parts required. If you can't articulate the mechanism, you don't understand the problem well enough.

**Step 3: Test cheaply.** Find the smallest experiment that validates or invalidates the hypothesis. Can you test with 10 users before building for 10,000? Can you mock it up before coding it? The cost of testing is always lower than the cost of building wrong.

**Step 4: Measure honestly.** Did the change produce the predicted result? If yes, scale. If no, diagnose why. The discipline of honest measurement separates learning organizations from self-deluding ones.

**Step 5: Ship or kill.** Ship what works. Kill what doesn't. The speed of this cycle — test, measure, decide — is the team's competitive advantage. Every week spent deliberating is a week the competitor spent learning.

## Real-World Application

**Pre-PMF (0-100 users):** At this stage, tier design craft is about finding signal in noise. The sample size is too small for statistical significance. Optimize for speed of learning: 5-10 experiments per month, each producing a clear insight. The founder should be directly involved in every tier design craft decision.

**Post-PMF, scaling (100-10K users):** tier design craft must be systematized — turned from founder instinct into repeatable process. Document what works. Hire someone to own it. Build the metrics dashboard. The danger is premature optimization — don't over-engineer before validating at 3-5x current scale.

**At scale (10K+ users):** tier design craft is an operational discipline with an owner, a dashboard, a review cadence, and a continuous improvement process. Decisions are about optimization (improving efficiency) not exploration (finding what works). Quarter-over-quarter improvement is the goal.

## Case Studies

Slack's freemium conversion: free tier is genuinely useful (no time limit, 90 days of message history), but enterprise teams hit the limit naturally as they grow. The conversion trigger is organic — teams outgrow free without Slack pushing them. This self-serve conversion model is why Slack's sales efficiency is among the highest in enterprise SaaS. Notion's packaging evolution: started with free-for-individuals, paid-for-teams. This aligned pricing with value — individuals get the tool for free (growth), teams pay for collaboration (revenue). The free tier builds the user base that eventually converts to team plans.

## Common Mistakes

The biggest mistake with tier design craft is treating it as a one-time decision instead of an ongoing discipline. Markets change. Users evolve. Competitors adjust. The team that reviews tier design craft quarterly outperforms the team that sets it and forgets it.

The second mistake is copying what worked for another company without adapting to your context. Frameworks transfer. Tactics don't. Your market, users, and product are unique. Use frameworks to derive your own approach.

The third mistake is measurement avoidance. If you can't measure whether tier design craft is working, you can't improve it. Define the metric before executing. If the metric doesn't move, the approach isn't working — regardless of how good it looks in the deck.

## The Product Craft Dimension

tier design craft is ultimately a craft skill — it improves with deliberate practice. The progression:

**Novice:** Applies frameworks mechanically. Follows the process. Gets decent results.

**Practitioner:** Adapts frameworks to context. Knows when to follow the process and when to deviate. Gets good results consistently.

**Expert:** Operates from internalized judgment. Sees patterns before they're obvious. Makes the right call under uncertainty and time pressure. Gets great results and teaches others how.

The path from novice to expert is ~2 years of deliberate practice: applying the frameworks, measuring the results, learning from the outcomes, and updating your judgment. There are no shortcuts. There are only more reps.

## When to use

When tier design craft is the current bottleneck or the highest-leverage opportunity. When user research, analytics, or competitive analysis surfaces this as a gap. When entering a new market, segment, or growth stage. When the current approach was set more than 6 months ago and hasn't been revisited.


## Advanced Monetization Craft

### The Pricing Decision Framework
Pricing decisions have three dimensions:

**The Model** — how you charge. Per seat, per usage, per feature tier, per outcome, flat rate, or hybrid. The model should align with how users perceive value. If value scales with users → per seat. If value scales with usage → usage-based. If value is binary (have it or don't) → flat rate.

**The Level** — how much you charge. Use value-based pricing: identify the economic value to the customer, price at 10-20% of that value. If your product saves a team of 5 people 10 hours/month each, and their time costs $75/hour, the value is $3,750/month. Price: $375-$750/month. Most startups underprice by 30-50% because they price on cost (what it costs to build) rather than value (what it's worth to the customer).

**The Structure** — how you package it. Good/Better/Best tiers work because they create anchoring (the expensive tier makes the middle tier feel reasonable) and self-selection (customers sort themselves by need). The middle tier should be the target — design it for your ICP and price the other tiers relative to it.

### Case Study: How Stripe Monetized Through Developer Experience
Stripe's pricing is straightforward (2.9% + 30¢ per transaction) — but their real monetization insight is making it easy to start AND hard to leave. The onboarding takes minutes (quick monetization start). But once a company has Stripe handling payments, subscriptions, invoicing, and fraud detection, the switching cost is enormous. Stripe's monetization strategy isn't price optimization — it's deepening the integration until the product becomes infrastructure. This is the strongest form of monetization: not charging more per transaction but making more transactions flow through your system.

### The Conversion Optimization Priority Stack
When optimizing conversion, work in this order (highest leverage first):

1. **Value proposition clarity** — does the visitor immediately understand what they get? (Headline, subheadline, hero image.) This has the highest impact because if the value isn't clear, nothing else matters.

2. **Trust signals** — does the visitor believe the value proposition? (Social proof, testimonials, logos, security badges.) Trust gaps are the #2 conversion killer after unclear value.

3. **CTA design** — is the desired action obvious and easy? (Button copy, placement, visual prominence.) A great value proposition with a hidden CTA still converts poorly.

4. **Friction reduction** — are there unnecessary barriers? (Form fields, account requirements, confusing navigation.) Each unnecessary step loses 10-20% of potential converts.

5. **Pricing presentation** — is the price framed as an investment, not a cost? (Value context, comparison, ROI framing.) Price objections often aren't about the number — they're about the framing.

Fix #1 before optimizing #5. Most teams optimize #3-5 while #1-2 are broken.
